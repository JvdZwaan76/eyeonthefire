Fire Prevention Guide

Protect your home and property with proven wildfire prevention strategies. Learn how to create defensible space, harden your home, and reduce fire risk through smart planning.

Start with Defensible Space Home Hardening Guide

Why Fire Prevention Matters

Research shows that homes with proper defensible space and fire-resistant features have up to a 85% better chance of surviving a wildfire. Most home ignitions during wildfires are caused by flying embers, not direct flame contact. By implementing these prevention strategies, you can dramatically increase your home's survivability.

Creating Defensible Space

Defensible space is the buffer you create between a building and the grass, trees, shrubs, or other wildland vegetation that surround it.

Zone 1 - Immediate Zone

0-30 feet from home

This is the most critical area for home protection. Focus on eliminating ignition sources and creating a lean, clean, and green environment.

Pro Tips:

  • Use irrigation to keep plants well-hydrated
  • Choose fire-resistant plant varieties
  • Maintain plants regularly - don't let them dry out

Zone 2 - Intermediate Zone

30-100 feet from home

Create fuel breaks to slow fire spread and reduce flame lengths. This zone should have widely spaced plants and trees.

Required Actions:

Pro Tips:

  • Group plants in small clusters separated by bare space
  • Use natural features like rocks as fuel breaks
  • Consider native, fire-resistant plant species
3

Zone 3 - Extended Zone

100-200 feet from home

This zone focuses on forest management and creating strategic fuel breaks to reduce fire intensity before it reaches your property.

Recommended Actions:

Pro Tips:

  • Work with neighbors on community-wide efforts
  • Consult with forestry professionals
  • Check local regulations before major tree removal

Home Hardening

Make your home more resistant to ignition by upgrading key components that are vulnerable to embers and radiant heat.

Roof & Gutters

High Vulnerability

The roof is often the first part of a home to ignite from flying embers. Proper roofing materials and maintenance are critical.

Best Materials

  • Class A Fire-Rated Composition shingle, metal, tile
  • Treated Wood Shingles With fire retardant treatment

Avoid These Materials

  • Untreated Wood Shakes Highly combustible

Action Items:

Vents

High Vulnerability

Vents are a primary entry point for embers. Upgrading to ember-resistant vents is one of the most cost-effective improvements.

Ember-Resistant Options

  • 1/4" Metal Mesh Corrosion-resistant
  • Intumescent Vents Close automatically when heated

Action Items:

Siding & Exterior Walls

Medium Vulnerability

Exterior walls face radiant heat and ember exposure. Fire-resistant materials and proper installation details are important.

Fire-Resistant Materials

  • Fiber Cement Non-combustible, durable
  • Stucco Over non-combustible sheathing
  • Treated Wood With fire-resistant treatment

Action Items:

Windows

Medium Vulnerability

Windows can break from radiant heat, allowing embers and flames to enter the home. Proper glazing and protection are essential.

Best Options

  • Dual-Pane Tempered Better heat resistance
  • Multi-Pane Windows Insulated glazing

Action Items:

Decks & Porches

High Vulnerability

Attached decks can provide a direct path for fire to reach the home. The area under decks is particularly vulnerable to ember accumulation.

Fire-Resistant Decking

  • Composite Decking Fire-resistant formulations
  • Pressure-Treated Lumber With fire-retardant treatment

Fire-Resistant Landscaping

Choose plants and design features that reduce fire risk while maintaining beautiful outdoor spaces.

Fire-Resistant Plant Characteristics

High Moisture Content

Plants that retain water in leaves and stems burn less readily

Low Resin/Oil Content

Avoid plants with high oil, resin, or wax content that ignite easily

Slow Growing

Plants that grow slowly typically have less combustible tissue

Low Maintenance

Plants that don't require frequent pruning reduce debris accumulation

Plants to Avoid

High Fire Hazard Plants

  • Juniper - High oil content, burns intensely
  • Pine Trees - Resinous, drop flammable needles
  • Eucalyptus - Extremely flammable oils
  • Pampas Grass - Dries out completely, burns rapidly
  • Bamboo - Dry hollow stems burn very hot

Problematic Characteristics

  • Plants that accumulate dead material
  • Plants with peeling or fibrous bark
  • Plants that produce a lot of litter
  • Plants with high oil or resin content
  • Plants that grow in very dense stands

Seasonal Maintenance Calendar

Year-round maintenance schedule to keep your fire prevention measures effective

Spring

March - May

Key Tasks:

  • Clean gutters and roof of debris
  • Prune dead branches from trees and shrubs
  • Check and clean vents
  • Test irrigation systems
  • Plan landscape improvements
  • Review and update evacuation plans

Summer

June - August

Key Tasks:

  • Mow grass regularly (keep under 4 inches)
  • Water plants regularly to maintain moisture
  • Remove dry and dead vegetation weekly
  • Check fire extinguishers and emergency kits
  • Monitor fire weather forecasts
  • Maintain wide access for firefighters

Fall

September - November

Key Tasks:

  • Rake and remove fallen leaves regularly
  • Final major pruning before winter
  • Clean gutters frequently during leaf drop
  • Harvest and store garden produce
  • Prepare emergency supplies for winter
  • Schedule professional tree inspections

Winter

December - February

Key Tasks:

  • Plan next year's landscape projects
  • Research and order fire-resistant plants
  • Review home insurance policies
  • Attend community preparedness meetings
  • Service and maintain tools and equipment
  • Update emergency contact information
