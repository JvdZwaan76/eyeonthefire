If you are in immediate danger, call 911 and evacuate immediately

1

Call 911

Report the fire location and request emergency assistance. Give your exact location and number of people present.

2

Evacuate Immediately

Leave now if ordered or if you feel threatened. Don't wait for official evacuation orders if you're in immediate danger.

3

Follow Evacuation Routes

Use predetermined evacuation routes. If blocked, turn around and find an alternative route. Never drive through flames or heavy smoke.

4

Stay Connected

Keep your phone charged and monitor emergency alerts. Text "HELP" to 911 if you can't make voice calls.

Emergency Kit Essentials

Pack these items in advance and keep your kit easily accessible. Plan for at least 3 days of supplies.

Water & Food

1 gallon water per person per day

Medical & Safety

Include prescription medications

Tools & Electronics

Keep batteries fresh

Documents & Money

Keep in waterproof container

Pro Tips

  • Store your emergency kit in a easily accessible location
  • Check expiration dates every 6 months
  • Keep a smaller "go bag" ready for immediate evacuation
  • Consider the needs of elderly family members or those with disabilities
  • Include comfort items for children (toys, books, games)

During a Wildfire

What to do when a wildfire is approaching or threatening your area

If You're at Home

1
Monitor alerts

Stay tuned to emergency radio or alerts on your phone

2
Prepare to evacuate

Have your emergency kit and important items ready

3
Close all windows and doors

Remove lightweight curtains and shut off gas valves

4
Water your roof and surroundings

If time permits and it's safe to do so

If You're in a Vehicle

1
Don't drive through flames

Turn around and find an alternative route

2
If trapped by fire

Park away from vegetation, close windows, stay low

3
Call 911

Report your location and situation

4
Cover yourself

Use floor mats or clothing to protect from radiant heat

If You're Outdoors

1
Find a safe area

Look for areas with sparse vegetation or bodies of water

2
Stay low to ground

Smoke and heat rise, so get as low as possible

3
Cover nose and mouth

Use clothing or wet cloth to filter smoke

4
Call for help

Signal your location with bright colors or noise

Protecting Yourself from Smoke

Wildfire smoke can be dangerous to your health even from fires miles away

Health Effects of Smoke

Mild Effects

  • Eye irritation
  • Runny nose
  • Mild cough
  • Phlegm production

Moderate Effects

  • Shortness of breath
  • Wheezing
  • Chest tightness
  • Heart palpitations

Severe Effects

  • Severe breathing difficulty
  • Chest pain
  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Dizziness or fainting

Protection Strategies

Indoor Protection

  • Keep windows and doors closed
  • Use air purifiers with HEPA filters
  • Create a "clean room" with minimal air leaks
  • Avoid activities that create more particles (smoking, candles)
  • Use air conditioning on recirculate mode

Personal Protection

  • Use N95 or P100 masks when outdoors
  • Limit outdoor activities, especially exercise
  • Stay indoors when air quality is poor
  • Seek medical attention if symptoms worsen
  • Follow local air quality alerts

After the Fire

Recovery and safety steps to take once the immediate threat has passed

Immediate

First 24-48 Hours

  • Wait for official all-clear before returning home
  • Be cautious of hazards like hot ash, damaged power lines
  • Check with authorities about water safety
  • Document damage with photos for insurance
  • Contact your insurance company as soon as possible
First Week

Initial Recovery

  • Begin cleanup only after getting professional assessment
  • Hire certified contractors for major damage
  • Keep receipts for all fire-related expenses
  • Contact FEMA if federal disaster declared
  • Reach out to local disaster relief organizations
Long-term

Long-term Recovery

  • Consider counseling for trauma and stress
  • Review and update your emergency plan
  • Implement fire-resistant improvements when rebuilding
  • Join community recovery and preparedness groups
  • Help neighbors and share lessons learned
